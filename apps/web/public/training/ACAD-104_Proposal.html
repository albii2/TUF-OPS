1.1 What a TUF Proposal Is

A TUF proposal is NOT a price quote with logos, a catalog page, or something you email and hope they read.

A TUF proposal IS:

  • The visual representation of their program's future identity
  • A carefully constructed presentation that shows them what they COULD look like
  • A document that leads with vision, anchors with standards, closes on the guarantee
  • A conversation tool — not a document meant to stand alone

BSN OBJECTIONS AT PROPOSAL TIME

The proposal stage is where BSN's dominance shows up hardest. Here's how to win:

"Coach, here's the difference between BSN's proposal and ours: BSN is showing you a Nike catalog with their markup. We're showing you TUF-built product — same quality standard, 30-40% less cost, with an IRONCLAD GUARANTEE they can't offer because they're a reseller. And they're showing you one lane — uniforms. We're showing you four lanes that build your program's identity."
MIDWEST PROPOSAL EDGE: Mention Minnesota-specific examples: "Coach, Edina football just switched — their kids feel the difference in PRO CUT fit. Minnetonka basketball uses OVERTIME — FLEX-WAIST solved their girls' reversible issue. Wayzata's whole program travels in ISSUE gear. These are programs like yours, in your conference, making the switch."

1.1 The Proposal Mindset

Principle 1: The Proposal Validates the Discovery

Every element connects back to what the coach told you. "Coach, you mentioned the waistbands on your shorts bunch up. That's why OVERTIME features FLEX-WAIST." The proposal should feel like you were listening — because you were.

Principle 2: Vision Before Numbers

Never lead with price. Never put price on page 1. The coach needs to see what their program looks like and understand what they're getting before they see what it costs.

Principle 3: Three Standards, Not Seven — THE 3-THING CLOSE

In every proposal, deploy exactly three quality standards: PRO CUT (lead), the sport-specific standard, and IRONCLAD GUARANTEE (close). More than three and the coach stops listening.

Principle 4: The Mockup Is the Star

The coach should spend 80% of the proposal conversation looking at the mockup — not the price sheet.

Principle 5: End with a Question, Not a Price

The closing line: "Coach, if your athletes walked out in these on Friday night, would you be proud of how they look?" Not "So what do you think about the numbers?"

1.3 The Golden Rule

Never send a proposal without scheduling the conversation to walk through it. A proposal sent by email without a conversation is a document that gets filed and forgotten.

3.1 The Pricing Conversation Sequence

Step 1: Recap Value (Before Numbers)

"Coach, before I show you numbers, let me recap what we're building. PRO CUT fit — college spec. [Sport standard] — so your athletes [benefit]. IRONCLAD GUARANTEE — if anything goes wrong, we fix it. Period. [Qty] uniforms for [number] athletes. That's what we're building."

Step 2: Present the Number (Clean and Direct)

"The investment for this program is [Total Price]."

Then stop talking. Let the number land. Don't rush to justify. Don't apologize. Don't say "but we can work with you on that." Present it and wait.

Step 3: Frame the Value (After They React)

If they react with concern:

"Coach, let me break that down. [Qty] uniforms for [number] athletes — that's [per-athlete price] per athlete. Built to last 3 seasons — about [per-season price] per season. Includes IRONCLAD GUARANTEE. The question isn't whether it's the cheapest option. It's whether the value — sharp fit, guaranteed delivery, uniform still sharp in Year 3 — is worth the difference."

3.3 Pricing Rules

  • Never discount. Discounting signals the price was inflated. Demonstrate value.
  • Never apologize for the price. "I know it's a lot" undermines everything. Own it.
  • Never rush through pricing. The price deserves the same confidence as the product.
  • Never negotiate against yourself. If the coach says "that's high," ask: "Compared to what?"
  • The team store is your pricing ally. It transforms "we can't afford it" into "let's figure out how to fund it."

4.1 Why Propose Multiple Lanes

A football coach who only buys GRIND can leave TUF next season. A coach whose entire program runs on TUF — uniforms, travel, team store, letterman jackets — is locked into a system no other vendor can replicate.

4.2 The Lane Expansion Conversation

During uniform proposal:

"Coach, while we're building GRIND uniforms — how does your program travel to away games? ISSUE travel gear — tech suits, duffles, backpacks — same quality standard, same design language. One system. Your kids compete in TUF and travel in TUF."

After uniform close:

"Coach, one more thing. Your community sees your athletes in GRIND on Friday nights. They want to rep the program too. Our team store — online storefront — lets them do that, and your program earns 15–25% on every sale. That's revenue you're leaving on the table."

"That's double what we're paying. I can't justify that to my AD."

Rep's Task: Don't discount. Reframe as investment. Per-athlete. Per-season. Team store offset.

Coach: "I love it. But my AD has final say, and she's been with BSN for 10 years."

Rep's Task: Offer to co-present. Arm coach with one-page summary. Don't go around the coach.

Scenario 4: The Comparison Shopper

Coach: "Nike sent me a proposal too. It's a little more, but... it's Nike."

Rep's Task: Acknowledge respectfully. Compare specific elements (fit, guarantee, 4-lane).

Scenario 5: The Silent Coach

Coach: Says almost nothing. Hard to read. One-word answers.

Rep's Task: Open-ended questions. Use silence strategically. Don't fill the void with chatter.

Scenario 6: "I Need to Think About It"

Coach: "This is a lot. Let me think about it and get back to you."

Rep's Task: Diagnose the real objection. "What specifically do you need to think through?"

Scenario 7: The Booster Club Barrier

Coach: "Boosters control the budget. They meet next month."

Rep's Task: Offer to present at booster meeting. Provide materials.

Scenario 8: Multi-Sport Opportunity

Coach: (Football coach) "These GRIND uniforms are great. You know, our basketball coach has been complaining too."

Scenario 9: Last-Minute Objection

## KNOWLEDGE ASSESSMENT — MODULE 04

Scenario-Based Questions

A proposal isn't a document — it's a conversation. These scenarios test whether you can navigate that conversation when it gets real.

SCENARIO 1: The Price Shock — Reframe in Real Time

Situation: You've just presented the investment amount to a football coach: $7,800 for a full varsity GRIND uniform set. He stares at the number. Then: "That's almost double what we're paying now. I can't take that to my AD. She'd laugh me out of the office." He's not saying no. He's saying the number scares him — and he doesn't know how to sell it internally.

Question: What's your verbatim response that (a) acknowledges the sticker shock without apologizing, (b) deploys at least two different pricing frames (per-athlete, per-season, vs. competitor, revenue offset), and (c) equips him to present this to his AD — not you presenting, HIM presenting?

SCENARIO 2: The "I Need to Think About It" Stall — Diagnose the Real Objection

Situation: You've walked through the full proposal with a basketball coach. The mockup looked great. The FLEX-WAIST demo landed. She's nodding. Then: "This is a lot to process. Let me think about it and get back to you." You know "I need to think about it" is rarely about needing more time — it's a cover for an unspoken objection.

Question: What's your diagnostic response that (a) validates her need to think, (b) asks the specific question that surfaces the REAL objection, and (c) keeps the conversation moving forward regardless of what she reveals? Write the exact words, including what you do after she answers.

SCENARIO 3: The Product Application Question

Situation: You're building a proposal for a multi-sport AD at a Tier 1 suburban school with 14 sports. Your discovery revealed: football is the flagship program (needs premium), basketball has waistband complaints (FLEX-WAIST opportunity), the travel gear is a mess of random bags, and there's no team store. Budget is decent but not unlimited.

Question: Design the proposal strategy. Which lane do you lead with? Which option from the bundle pricing (Section 11.2) do you recommend as the first proposal — and what's the specific language you'd use to present three options (Core, Full Program, Program Identity) to this AD?
