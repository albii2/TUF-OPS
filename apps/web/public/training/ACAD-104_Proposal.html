PART 1: THE PROPOSAL MINDSET

1.1 What a TUF Proposal Is

A TUF proposal is NOT a price quote with logos, a catalog page, or something you email and hope they read. A TUF proposal IS:

  • The visual representation of their program's future identity
  • A carefully constructed presentation that shows them what they COULD look like
  • A document that leads with vision, anchors with standards, closes on the guarantee
  • A conversation tool — not a document meant to stand alone

1.2 The Proposal Principles

Principle 1: The Proposal Validates the Discovery — Every element connects back to what the coach told you.

Principle 2: Vision Before Numbers — Never lead with price. Never put price on page 1.

Principle 3: Three Standards, Not Seven — THE 3-THING CLOSE — Deploy exactly three quality standards: PRO CUT (lead), the sport-specific standard, and IRONCLAD GUARANTEE (close).

Principle 4: The Mockup Is the Star — The coach should spend 80% of the proposal conversation looking at the mockup — not the price sheet.

Principle 5: End with a Question, Not a Price — The closing line: "Coach, if your athletes walked out in these on Friday night, would you be proud of how they look?"

1.3 The Golden Rule

Never send a proposal without scheduling the conversation to walk through it. A proposal sent by email without a conversation is a document that gets filed and forgotten.

PART 3: PRICING PRESENTATION — INVESTMENT, NOT EXPENSE

3.1 The Pricing Conversation Sequence

Step 1: Recap Value (Before Numbers)

"Coach, before I show you numbers, let me recap what we're building. PRO CUT fit — college spec. [Sport standard] — so your athletes [benefit]. IRONCLAD GUARANTEE — if anything goes wrong, we fix it. Period."

Step 2: Present the Number (Clean and Direct)

"The investment for this program is [Total Price]."

Then stop talking. Let the number land. Don't rush to justify. Don't apologize.

Step 3: Frame the Value (After They React)

"Coach, let me break that down. [Qty] uniforms for [number] athletes — that's [per-athlete price] per athlete. Built to last 3 seasons — about [per-season price] per season. Includes IRONCLAD GUARANTEE."

3.2 Pricing Frames

FrameLanguageWhen to Use
Per-Athlete"That's $[X] per athlete. Less than a pair of cleats."Budget-sensitive coaches.
Per-Season"Over 3 years, that's $[X] per season."When comparing to annual replacements.
Vs. College"Same fit standard at [College] costs $[X] per uniform."Against Nike/Adidas.
Vs. Current Spend"You're paying $[X] now and dealing with [pain]. Worth $[difference]?"Coach mentions current vendor price.
Revenue Offset"Your team store can generate $[X]/year — offsetting a significant portion."Budget is primary objection.

3.3 Pricing Rules

  1. Never discount. Discounting signals the price was inflated. Demonstrate value.
  2. Never apologize for the price. "I know it's a lot" undermines everything.
  3. Never rush through pricing. The price deserves the same confidence as the product.
  4. Never negotiate against yourself. If the coach says "that's high," ask: "Compared to what?"
  5. The team store is your pricing ally. It transforms "we can't afford it" into "let's figure out how to fund it."

PART 4: THE 4-LANE EXPANSION PROPOSAL

4.1 Why Propose Multiple Lanes

A football coach who only buys GRIND can leave TUF next season. A coach whose entire program runs on TUF — uniforms, travel, team store, letterman jackets — is locked into a system no other vendor can replicate.

4.2 The Lane Expansion Conversation

During uniform proposal:

"Coach, while we're building GRIND uniforms — how does your program travel to away games? ISSUE travel gear — tech suits, duffles, backpacks — same quality standard, same design language. One system."

After uniform close:

"Coach, one more thing. Your community sees your athletes in GRIND on Friday nights. They want to rep the program too. Our team store — online storefront — lets them do that, and your program earns 15–25% on every sale."

8.2 The Demo Sequence

1. PRO CUT FIT (Lead — every demo)
   → Hold TUF jersey. Show taper through torso.
   → Point to articulated sleeves.
   → "PRO CUT fit. Built to what college programs wear."

2. SPORT-SPECIFIC STANDARD
   → Basketball: Flip FLEX-WAIST waistband. Clean. 2 seconds.
     "FLEX-WAIST. Engineered to stay flat."
   → Football: Stretch POWER-STRETCH in 4 directions.
     "POWER-STRETCH. Moves with the athlete. Snaps back."
   → Heat sports: Hold AIR-FORGE to light. Show perforations.

3. IRONCLAD GUARANTEE (Close — always last)
   → Eye contact. Deliberate pause.
   → "IRONCLAD GUARANTEE. If anything goes wrong, we fix it.
     Period. Not pro-rated. Not 'within reason.' Fixed."

8.3 Demo Rules

  1. Always hand the sample to the coach. Ownership begins in the hands.
  2. 60% action, 40% words. Let the product speak.
  3. Mirror interest. Coach leans in → go deeper. Distracted → keep it moving.
  4. Never more than 3 standards. More than 3 and absorption stops.
  5. End every demo with IRONCLAD GUARANTEE. It's the lasting impression.

PART 9: HANDLING THE "WE NEED TO THINK ABOUT IT" STALL

9.1 What It Really Means

TranslationYour Move
"Price scared me."Reframe per-athlete, per-season. Introduce team store offset.
"I don't have authority."Offer to present to decision-maker. Arm them with a summary.
"I'm comparing vendors."Give comparison criteria: PRO CUT vs. stock blank.
"Something felt off.""What specifically do you need to think through?"
"Timing isn't right.""When's the right time? Let's put it on the calendar."

9.2 The Diagnostic Response

"Coach, I completely understand — this is an important decision. What specifically do you need to think through? Price, fit, timeline — or something else? I want to make sure I've answered everything."

Then LISTEN. Don't fill the silence. The coach will tell you the real objection.

9.3 The Graceful Exit

"Coach, it sounds like the timing isn't right — I respect that. The proposal doesn't expire. If anything changes — budget frees up, vendor lets you down — I'm here. I'll check in with you [specific timeframe]. Best of luck with the season."

PART 13: 15 PROPOSAL ROLE-PLAYS

Scenario 1: The Enthusiastic Yes

Coach: "These look incredible. The kids will lose their minds. What do I need to do?"
Task: Don't oversell. Move smoothly to order handoff. Set expectations.

Scenario 2: The Price Shock

Coach: "That's double what we're paying. I can't justify that to my AD."
Task: Don't discount. Reframe as investment. Per-athlete. Per-season. Team store offset.

Scenario 3: The AD Blocker

Coach: "I love it. But my AD has final say, and she's been with BSN for 10 years."
Task: Offer to co-present. Arm coach with one-page summary.

Scenario 4: The Comparison Shopper

Coach: "Nike sent me a proposal too. It's a little more, but... it's Nike."
Task: Acknowledge respectfully. Compare specific elements (fit, guarantee, 4-lane).

Scenario 5: The Silent Coach

Coach: Says almost nothing. Hard to read. One-word answers.
Task: Open-ended questions. Use silence strategically.

Scenario 6: "I Need to Think About It"

Coach: "This is a lot. Let me think about it and get back to you."
Task: Diagnose the real objection. "What specifically do you need to think through?"

Scenario 7: The Booster Club Barrier

Coach: "Boosters control the budget. They meet next month."
Task: Offer to present at booster meeting. Provide materials.

Scenario 8: Multi-Sport Opportunity

Coach: "These GRIND uniforms are great. You know, our basketball coach has been complaining too."
Task: Ask for introduction to basketball coach. Don't let it pass.

Scenario 9: Last-Minute Objection

Coach: "Wait — what if the colors don't match our helmets?"
Task: Address specific concern. PMS color matching. Reassure.

Scenario 10: Timeline Concern

Coach: "We need these by August 1. Can you guarantee that?"
Task: Be honest about production timelines. Don't overpromise.

KNOWLEDGE ASSESSMENT — MODULE 04

Scenario-Based Questions

A proposal isn't a document — it's a conversation. These scenarios test whether you can navigate that conversation when it gets real.

SCENARIO 1: The Price Shock

Situation: You've just presented the investment amount to a football coach: $7,800 for a full varsity GRIND uniform set. He stares at the number. Then: "That's almost double what we're paying now. I can't take that to my AD."

Question: What's your verbatim response that (a) acknowledges the sticker shock without apologizing, (b) deploys at least two different pricing frames, and (c) equips him to present this to his AD?

SCENARIO 2: The "I Need to Think About It" Stall

Situation: You've walked through the full proposal with a basketball coach. The mockup looked great. The FLEX-WAIST demo landed. She's nodding. Then: "This is a lot to process. Let me think about it and get back to you."

Question: What's your diagnostic response that surfaces the REAL objection and keeps the conversation moving forward?

SCENARIO 3: The Product Application Question

Situation: You're building a proposal for a multi-sport AD at a Tier 1 suburban school with 14 sports. Football is the flagship. Basketball has waistband complaints. Travel gear is a mess. There's no team store.

Question: Design the proposal strategy. Which lane do you lead with? Which bundle option do you recommend as the first proposal?

SCENARIO 4: "Nike Sent Me a Proposal Too"

Situation: The coach says: "I appreciate this. But Nike sent me a proposal too. It's a little more expensive, but... it's Nike. How do I justify going with a company nobody's heard of?"

Question: What's your response that acknowledges Nike's brand strength, gives side-by-side comparison criteria, and ends on TUF differentiators Nike literally cannot match?

Recall Foundation

  1. The 7 Proposal Components: Name all seven in order.
  2. The 3-Call Close: What happens on each call, and what's the timing?
  3. Pricing Rules: What are the five pricing rules?

Director Checkpoint

  • Full Proposal Walkthrough: You deliver a proposal for a football coach. Director evaluates: flow, confidence during pricing moment, physical demo quality.
  • Price Objection Drill: Director throws a price objection. You respond with framing, not discounting.
  • The Closing Question: You deliver "If your athletes walked out in these..." Then Director stays silent for 10+ seconds. You must NOT fill the silence.
