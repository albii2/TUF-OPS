1.1 What Discovery Is

Discovery is NOT a pitch. It's NOT a demo.

Discovery is understanding a coach's program so deeply that when you present a solution, it feels inevitable — not like a sale.

A great discovery call accomplishes four things:

  • You understand the program — size, levels, sports, culture, current state
  • The coach feels understood — probably for the first time by a vendor
  • You've uncovered pain — the specific frustrations that create urgency
  • You've built enough trust — the coach wants to see what you have

1.2 The Discovery Call Rules

Rule 1: First 10 Minutes = Zero TUF, All Program

Most vendors start by talking about themselves. TUF starts by asking about the coach's program. And keeps asking.

Rule 2: Listen 80%, Talk 20%

For the first 15 minutes, if you're talking more than the coach, you're pitching — not discovering.

Rule 3: Don't Interrupt Pain

When a coach vents about their current vendor, let them. Don't jump in with solutions. Don't say "that's exactly why we built TUF." The more they articulate their frustration, the more they sell themselves on change.

Rule 4: Follow Emotion, Not Just Facts

"12 kids on varsity" is a fact. "I'm tired of watching my seniors wear boxy jerseys in their last team photo" is emotion. Discovery follows both.

Rule 5: The Coach Does 70% of the Talking

If you did most of the talking in a 20-minute discovery call, you failed. The coach should walk away thinking: "That rep really listened."

1.3 Discovery Call Structure

MINUTE 0–2      GREETING + RAPPORT
                "Coach, thanks for the time. I was looking at
                [School]'s [sport] and I had some questions."
MINUTE 2–5      SITUATION QUESTIONS
                Program size, levels, current setup. Broad. Factual. Safe.
MINUTE 5–10     PROBLEM QUESTIONS
                What's not working? What would you change?
                This is where pain surfaces.
MINUTE 10–13    IMPLICATION QUESTIONS
                What if it continues? What's the cost of doing nothing?
                This creates urgency.
MINUTE 13–15    NEED-PAYOFF QUESTIONS
                What if it were solved? What would that mean?

2.1 What SPIN Is

SPIN (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff) is one of the most validated sales methodologies in history. Developed by Neil Rackham from analysis of 35,000 sales calls.

TUF ACADEMY  //  ACAD-103  //  BUILT FOR THE PROGRAM
TUF
Question Type Purpose Example
SITUATION Understand current reality "How many kids in your program?"
PROBLEM Uncover dissatisfaction "What's your biggest frustration with your uniforms?"
IMPLICATION Make the pain real "What happens if uniforms arrive late for the season opener?"
NEED-PAYOFF Articulate the value of solving "If the fit was sharp and delivery guaranteed, what would that mean?"

2.2 SPIN Rules

Do Don't
✅ Ask open-ended questions ❌ Ask leading questions that point to TUF
✅ Let silence sit — the coach will fill it ❌ Jump in with solutions at first sign of pain
✅ Follow emotional cues ❌ Stick rigidly to your question list
✅ Use "tell me more" when they reveal pain ❌ Say "we can fix that" before understanding fully
✅ Sequence: S → P → I → N ❌ Skip to Need-Payoff before establishing real problems

# Context Need-Payoff Question
1 After discussing boxy fit "If your athletes walked out in uniforms that fit like college players wear — sharp taper — what would that do for their confidence?"
2 After discussing vendor unreliability "Imagine one call solved any problem — and problems got fixed, period. What would it mean to not worry about uniforms?"

7.1 Budget Discovery Questions

TUF ACADEMY  //  ACAD-103  //  BUILT FOR THE PROGRAM
TUF
# Question What You're Learning
1 "How does uniform funding work in your district? School board, boosters, or mix?" Budget source.
2 "What's the ballpark you're working with for uniforms this season?" Budget range. Which tier to propose.
3 "What are you paying per uniform right now?" Comparison anchor.
4 "Has your uniform budget changed in the last few years? Up, down, flat?" Budget trend.
5 "When does the budget get finalized? Who approves?" If locked, sell for next cycle. If open, urgency.
6 "Do your boosters contribute? How active is the booster club?" Active boosters can supplement.

7.2 Pricing Conversation Rules

Never discuss specific pricing until the coach has seen the value.

TUF ACADEMY  //  ACAD-103  //  BUILT FOR THE PROGRAM
TUF
Timing What to Say
Coach asks price early (before demo) "Coach, I want to give you an accurate number based on what your program actually needs — not a generic price. Let me learn more first, then I'll put together something specific."
Coach presses on price "I understand budget is real. Let me show you what you're getting — PRO CUT fit, [sport standard], IRONCLAD GUARANTEE — then we'll talk numbers. Fair?"
Coach says "just give me a ballpark" "For a program your size, most schools invest between $X and $Y for a full uniform set. But that depends on exactly what you need. Let me put together something specific for [School]."

8.3 Multi-Stakeholder Strategy

  • Identify the champion. Who wants change the most?
  • Arm the champion. Give them talking points, a sample, a one-page summary.
  • Offer to present to the group. "Coach, I'd be happy to do 15 minutes for you and the AD."
  • Don't go around your champion. If the coach says the AD decides, ask for an introduction.

10.1 Beyond the Initial Sport

You might be talking to a football coach. But that school also has basketball, baseball, softball, volleyball, and more. Every sport is a potential lane. Every coach is a champion in a different part of the department.

10.2 Multi-Sport Discovery Questions

TUF ACADEMY  //  ACAD-103  //  BUILT FOR THE PROGRAM
TUF
# Question Opportunity
1 "What other sports are big at [School]? Another program that could benefit?" Opens door to other sports.
2 "Does basketball use the same vendor as football, or do they do their own thing?" Two-sport opportunity.
3 "Is there a girls' sport that's been asking for better uniforms?" Women's Division. FLEX-WAIST is powerful for girls' basketball.
4 "Do lower levels — JV, freshman, middle school — get new uniforms or hand-me-downs?" Volume opportunity.
5 "When your athletes travel, what are they wearing? Coordinated gear?" Lane 2 (ISSUE). Fastest cross-sell.

12.1 When to Transition

Ready signals:

  • Coach mentioned a specific frustration you can solve
  • Coach asked "so what do you do differently?"
  • Coach expressed curiosity: "What's your story?"
  • Coach acknowledged change might be worth considering
  • Tone shifted from defensive to open

NOT ready signals:

  • Short, guarded answers
  • No frustration revealed
  • "We're happy with what we have" — and meant it
  • Distracted, trying to get off the phone

12.2 The Energy Shift Script

"Coach, you just told me three things: [pain 1], [pain 2], and you wish you had [need-payoff]. Here's why we should talk further: TUF was built to solve exactly what you're describing."

Then deploy the 4-lane moat:

"We don't sell uniforms. We outfit programs. Four lanes — game day, travel, fans, tradition. One system. One look. One relationship. Nobody else thinks about your program that way."

Then introduce the fit differentiator:

"And everything we build — PRO CUT fit to college spec, LOCK-FIT construction, IRONCLAD GUARANTEE — is designed to solve the exact frustrations you described."

Then transition to the demo:

"Let me show you what this looks like. I've got a sample right here..."

12.3 If the Coach Isn't Ready

"Coach, I've learned a lot about your program — thank you. I don't want to pitch you if the timing isn't right. Let me leave you my card and a sample. If anything changes with your current situation, I'm here. No pressure."

The sample is the Trojan horse. It sells when you're not in the room.

Scenario 1: The Satisfied Coach

Coach: Happy with Nike. No complaints.

"Honestly, we love Nike. Fit is good, delivery reliable, kids love the swoosh."

Model Flow:

  • Situation: "Tell me about your program."
  • Problem: "So everything's working. Anything you'd change if you could?"
  • Implication: Skip — no pain to amplify.
  • Need-Payoff: "What if one vendor could handle uniforms, travel, team stores, and letterman jackets — one system?"
  • Energy Shift: "Coach, I'm not here to break a good thing. Let me leave you a GRIND sample. If anything changes, I'm here."

Scenario 2: The Frustrated Basketball Coach

Coach: Girls' Basketball. Uniforms boxy. Waistbands bunch. Graphics peeling.

"These uniforms are terrible. The girls hate them. Waistbands are a disaster."

Model Flow:

  • Situation: "Tell me about your program."
  • Problem: "What's the biggest frustration?"
  • (Coach vents about waistbands.)
  • Implication: "How many times in a game does a player adjust her shorts? What does that do to her focus?"
  • Need-Payoff: "If the waistband stayed flat — no bunching — what would that mean?"
  • Energy Shift: "Coach, you just described exactly what FLEX-WAIST solves. Let me bring you OVERTIME samples this week."

Scenario 3: The Time-Pressed AD

Coach: AD. 14 sports. 5 minutes.

"I've got 5 minutes. What do I need to know?"

Model Flow:

  • Situation (30 sec): "How many sports? Biggest uniform challenge?"
  • Problem (1 min): "Most frustrating part of managing uniforms for 14 sports?"
  • Implication (1 min): "Managing 4 vendors — what's that costing you in time?"
  • Need-Payoff (30 sec): "One vendor, four lanes, one call — would that simplify things?"
  • Energy Shift (1 min): "That's exactly what we do. 15 minutes this week?"

Scenario 4: The "We Have No Money" Coach

Coach: Small rural school. Zero budget. 6-year-old uniforms.

"We have zero budget. Booster club raises maybe $500 a year."

Model Flow:

  • Situation: "Tell me about your program."
  • Problem: "What's the condition of the current uniforms?"
  • Implication: "How do the girls feel wearing 6-year-old uniforms when other teams have new gear?"
  • Need-Payoff: "What if there were a way to fund new uniforms without touching your school budget?"
  • Energy Shift: "Coach, our team store model. Your program earns 15–25% on every sale. Some programs fund their entire uniform order this way. I'll send you the info. No pressure."

Scenario 5: The Multi-Sport AD

Coach: AD at large suburban school. 16 sports. Overwhelmed by vendor management.

"Managing all these vendors is a part-time job. But how can one company do 16 sports well?"

Model Flow:

  • Situation: "16 sports. Walk me through how you manage all the vendors."
  • Problem: "Biggest headache of 4+ vendor relationships?"
  • Implication: "How much of your week goes to vendor management vs. leading the department?"
  • Need-Payoff: "One vendor with sport-specific expertise — what would that change?"
  • Energy Shift: "We don't try to do 'everything.' Four specific lanes — GRIND for football, OVERTIME for basketball, DIAMOND for baseball/softball, ISSUE travel, LEGACY jackets. Each lane has expertise. One standard. One relationship. Start with one sport. If we deliver, expand."

## KNOWLEDGE ASSESSMENT — MODULE 03

Scenario-Based Questions

Discovery isn't about asking the right questions from a list — it's about knowing which question to ask NEXT based on what the coach just said.

SCENARIO 1: The Hidden Pain Point

Situation: You're on a discovery call with a head football coach. You ask: "How's the fit on your current uniforms?" He says: "They're fine. No complaints." But two minutes later, when you ask about his team photo, he pauses and says: "...well, the sleeves do look a little boxy in pictures."

Question: He just revealed a pain point he initially denied. What's your NEXT question — using the SPIN framework — that moves this from "mild observation" to "motivation to change"? Be specific: is it a Problem, Implication, or Need-Payoff question?

SCENARIO 2: The Emotional Pivot

Situation: You're talking to a girls' basketball coach who's been venting for 3 minutes about her current vendor: late delivery, wrong sizes, peeling graphics. She's angry — the uniforms arrived 3 weeks late and her seniors played their first three games in old jerseys. She's practically selling herself on switching.

Question: She's given you plenty of Problem material. What's your NEXT SPIN move — and specifically, what Implication question do you ask that makes the pain about her ATHLETES, not about the vendor? The goal: shift from "I hate my vendor" to "my athletes deserve better."

SCENARIO 3: The Product Application Question

Situation: During discovery, a volleyball coach mentions three things: (1) her athletes complain the jerseys are boxy and unflattering, (2) the fabric doesn't breathe during tournament weekends when they play 4+ matches a day, and (3) she's tired of managing separate orders for uniforms, warmups, and spirit wear.

Question: Which THREE specific TUF solutions — one collection, one quality standard, and one lane — directly map to her three complaints? For each, write the one-sentence transition you'd use to introduce it during the Energy Shift.

SCENARIO 4: The Objection — "We're Happy with What We Have"

Situation: You're on a discovery call with a baseball coach who's been with the same local vendor for 12 years. Every Situation question gets a positive answer. Every Problem question gets "nope, they're great." He's genuinely satisfied — or at least thinks he is.

Question: You can't force pain that isn't there. What's your graceful exit strategy that (a) acknowledges his satisfaction, (b) leaves behind a physical reminder of TUF, and (c) positions you as the first call he makes if anything changes — without a single pushy sentence?

Recall Foundation

  • SPIN Framework: What does each letter stand for, and in what order should the question types generally flow during a discovery call?
  • Discovery Call Rules: What's the talk ratio rule (coach vs. rep) for the first 15 minutes?
  • Energy Shift: What are three signals that a coach is ready for the transition from discovery to demo?

Director Checkpoint

Complete a 15-minute role-play with your Director:

  • Full SPIN Sequence: Director plays a frustrated basketball coach. You execute Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-Payoff → Energy Shift. Director evaluates: question quality, listening (not jumping to solutions), natural flow.
  • The "No Pain" Coach: Director plays a satisfied football coach. You gracefully exit — acknowledge satisfaction, leave a sample, no pressure. Director evaluates: professionalism, seed-planting.
  • Multi-Sport Pivot: Mid-conversation, Director mentions the softball program is struggling. You pivot to multi-sport discovery without abandoning the current conversation.

Coaching Guide: How Your Director Will Use This Manual to Coach You
