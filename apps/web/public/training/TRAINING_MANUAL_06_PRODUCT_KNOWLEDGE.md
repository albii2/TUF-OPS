## PART 1: THE PRODUCT KNOWLEDGE MINDSET

### 1.1 Knowing Specs vs. Embodying the Brand

There's a difference between knowing what POWER-STRETCH is and being able to hold up a jersey, stretch the fabric in four directions, and make a coach feel the difference. The Academy tests both — but the second one is what closes deals.

| Knows Specs | Embodies the Brand |
|-------------|-------------------|
| "PRO CUT is our athletic-tapered fit with articulated sleeves and reduced seam volume." | *Holds up jersey next to competitor.* "Coach, see the difference? That's PRO CUT. Built to what college programs actually wear. Your athletes feel it the second they put it on." |
| "POWER-STRETCH is our 4-way stretch fabric with shape recovery." | *Stretches fabric in four directions.* "POWER-STRETCH. Moves with the athlete. Snaps back. Doesn't bag out after a season of Friday nights and laundry. Feel it." |
| "The 4-lane system includes uniforms, travel gear, team stores, and letterman jackets." | "Coach, right now you're juggling four vendors. We do all four. One system. One look. One call if anything goes wrong. That's the difference between buying uniforms and building your program's identity." |

**The Academy tests the second column. You should know the first column cold — but you deploy the second column in the field.**

### 1.2 The Product Knowledge Principles

**Principle 1: The Demo Proves the Claim**

Never say a standard name without showing it physically. "PRO CUT" means nothing without holding up a jersey and showing the taper. The words anchor the memory. The action proves the claim.

**Principle 2: Three Standards, Not Seven**

In any conversation, a rep deploys exactly three quality standards: PRO CUT (always first), the sport-specific standard, and IRONCLAD GUARANTEE (always last). The other four standards (POWER-STRETCH, AIR-FORGE, LOCK-FIT, PACKSYSTEM) are supporting tools — used when relevant, never crowding the conversation.

**Principle 3: Collections Are Identities, Not SKUs**

GRIND isn't "the football jersey." It's "what your football program wears when the work meets the field." Every collection has an energy, an attitude, a feeling. Learn the feeling — not just the name.

**Principle 4: The Founder Credential Lives in Product Knowledge**

PRO CUT exists because the founder saw how college programs cut their jerseys. Every quality standard traces back to that inside knowledge. When a rep deploys PRO CUT, they're deploying the founder's experience in the form of a product.

**Principle 5: Know What We Don't Claim**

TUF doesn't claim to have invented polyester, moisture-wicking, or sublimation printing. We claim to have quality standards — named thresholds that our vendors must meet. This honesty builds credibility. Claiming to have invented commodity processes destroys it.

## PART 4: THE 7 QUALITY STANDARDS — TECHNOLOGY REFERENCE

### 4.1 The Philosophy

TUF has seven branded quality standards. Each is a standard TUF demands from its vendors and delivers to its athletes — not a claim to have invented the underlying material or process.

This is exactly what Nike does with Dri-FIT (a moisture standard, not a polyester invention) and Adidas does with AEROREADY (a performance standard, not a fabric patent). TUF plays the same game — with the same legitimacy.

**Governing Rule:** If TUF can point to a specific, verifiable quality threshold that differentiates its product from a generic alternative, it deserves a name. If it's a universal commodity where TUF adds nothing beyond what every vendor delivers, it doesn't.

### 4.2 STANDARD 1 — PRO CUT™

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Category** | FIT |
| **Definition** | Athletic-tapered pattern with articulated sleeves, reduced seam volume. Built to specifications derived from inside knowledge of college/pro uniform patterns. |
| **15-Second Claim** | "Athletic-tapered fit built to the specifications of what college programs actually wear. Articulated sleeves. Reduced seam volume. Not a boxy stock blank." |
| **Physical Demo** | Hold TUF jersey next to competitor jersey (or current uniform). Show taper through torso. Show sleeve articulation — they angle forward, not straight out. |

## PART 6: CUSTOMIZATION OPTIONS

### 6.1 Decoration Methods

| Method | Process | Best For | Durability | Look |
|--------|---------|----------|------------|------|
| **Full Sublimation** | Ink is heat-transferred into the polyester fiber at a molecular level. Color becomes part of the fabric. | Complex designs, all-over patterns, gradients, unlimited colors | Excellent — won't peel, crack, or fade. Color is IN the fiber. | Clean, vibrant, unlimited design flexibility. |
| **Screen Printing** | Ink is applied to the surface of the fabric through a mesh screen. | Simple designs, solid colors, budget-conscious orders | Good — may crack or fade over time with heavy washing | Solid, bold colors. Slightly heavier feel. |
| **Tackle Twill** | Fabric letters/numbers are cut and stitched onto the jersey. | Premium varsity look, traditional programs | Excellent — stitched on, not printed | Heavy, raised, textured. "This is the show." |
| **Embroidery** | Thread is stitched directly into the fabric to create the design. | Logos on polos, hats, small applications | Excellent | Premium, textured, professional. |
| **Heat Transfer** | Design is printed on transfer paper and heat-pressed onto the fabric. | Names, numbers, small applications | Fair — may peel over time with heavy washing | Clean, smooth. Less durable than sublimation. |
